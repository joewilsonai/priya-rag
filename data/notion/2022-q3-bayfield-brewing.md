# Bayfield Brewing Co. — Engagement Notes (2022 Q3)

**Client:** Bayfield Brewing Co. — craft brewery, San Diego CA
**Engagement type:** Distribution-network redesign
**Duration:** 6 weeks (Jul 11 – Aug 19, 2022)
**Fee:** $58k
**Outcome:** Three-route redesign (SF, LA, Phoenix) reduced distribution cost per case by 14%

---

## What we did

Bayfield was running a single self-distribution route from San Diego covering SF, LA, and Phoenix on weekly cycles — sending one truck Monday morning, returning Friday evening. The brewery was breaking 3-day delivery promises 40% of the time and burning the relationships with mid-tier accounts (independent bottle shops, restaurant groups) where reliability mattered more than price.

I redesigned to a **three-route distinct cycle:**

- **LA route (highest density):** twice-weekly Monday/Thursday, owned-truck self-distribution; covers the 38 highest-volume LA-area accounts.
- **SF route (mid-density, 380mi away):** weekly Wednesday, owned-truck self-distribution; covers the 22 SF-area accounts.
- **Phoenix route (lowest density, 350mi away):** outsourced to a regional distributor (Solano Beverage). Lost some margin but recovered reliability — Solano was running daily Phoenix routes anyway.

## Quantified result

- Distribution cost per case: $4.20 → $3.62 (-14%)
- On-time delivery: 60% → 91% within 90 days of redesign
- Account churn (lost retail accounts due to delivery issues) dropped from 18% trailing-12 → 4%
- ROI on engagement: ~3x ($170k annualized savings on cost / $58k fee), but the real gain was the reliability story

## What I learned about route density

This engagement is the one I cite when teaching route-density concepts to other clients. **The lesson: density determines the distribution model.** High-density route = self-distribution wins. Low-density route = third-party distributor wins. Mid-density is the squishy middle where you have to decide based on margin tolerance + relationship importance. Bayfield's three routes hit the three classic patterns — that's why I keep referring back to it.

## What didn't go well

Owner (Sara Chen, founder) was emotionally attached to "we control distribution end-to-end" as a brand differentiator. The Phoenix outsourcing was hard for her. I made the case three times before she signed off. **Lesson: when distribution is part of the brand story for a small CPG owner, the data argument has to be paired with a brand-story argument that lets them keep telling the "we control the experience" story. For Bayfield, that became "we control distribution where we can do it better than anyone — LA and SF."**

## Stakeholders

- **Diego Vargas** (Operations Manager) — engagement co-lead; ran the route redesign rollout; ex-Anheuser-Busch route planner, knew the Phoenix-density math instantly.
