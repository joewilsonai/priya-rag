# Northbridge Aerospace — Engagement Notes (2024 Q2)

**Client:** Northbridge Aerospace — aerospace structural components, Hartford CT
**Engagement type:** ERP evaluation + selection
**Duration:** 14 weeks (Mar 4 – Jun 9, 2024)
**Fee:** $148k
**Outcome:** Recommended Infor LN; client selected NetSuite (over my recommendation); engagement closed civil but the post-mortem is rough

---

## What I recommended vs what they did

After 14 weeks of evaluation across 6 ERP candidates (SAP S/4HANA, Oracle NetSuite, Infor LN, Epicor Kinetic, IFS Cloud, IQMS), I recommended **Infor LN** as the strongest fit for Northbridge's process-manufacturing requirements + aerospace traceability needs. The 47-criteria scoring matrix had Infor LN at 84/100, with NetSuite at 71/100.

The Northbridge CFO (Holly Rasmussen — no relation to Carl Rasmussen at Westmont; common Cleveland-area surname) overrode the recommendation and selected NetSuite. Stated reasons: (a) "NetSuite is Oracle-backed, lower implementation risk," (b) NetSuite's pricing was 22% cheaper over 5 years on the table she received, (c) the COO (Tom Akerly) had implemented NetSuite at his previous company.

## Why I think it was the wrong call (off the record, in this note only)

Aerospace components require **process-manufacturing-grade traceability** — every step of every part has to be linked back to material lot, machine, operator, test result. NetSuite's process-manufacturing module is comparatively immature relative to Infor LN's aerospace lineage (Infor has aerospace OEM customers at Boeing-supplier scale; NetSuite's aerospace customers are mostly distributors). The pricing advantage was real but didn't account for ~$280k of customization work Northbridge will need to add the traceability features that ship native in Infor LN.

I documented all of this clearly in the recommendation deck and in a follow-up email to Holly. She acknowledged the trade-off and selected NetSuite anyway. That's her call to make. **My job was to provide clear-eyed analysis, not to win the argument.** I delivered that.

## Why the client relationship is at risk anyway

Six months after engagement close (Jan 2025), Tom Akerly emailed asking "do you have any thoughts on the NetSuite implementation challenges we're hitting?" I replied with a careful note offering to take a paid follow-on if they wanted my eye on it. Crickets since. **Read: they're hitting the customization-cost wall I predicted, and the air feels embarrassed.** Won't be a referenceable client.

## What I'd do differently

The fee was solid ($148k for 14 weeks is good rate). The work was solid. The client made the wrong call. The lesson isn't really about my work — it's about which engagements to take. **Mid-market aerospace with a CFO who already has a vendor preference baked in before evaluation kickoff is a high-risk engagement type.** Holly had hinted at NetSuite preference in Week 2; I should have either renegotiated the engagement to "implementation roadmap regardless of vendor selected" or declined.

## Stakeholders

- **Holly Rasmussen** (CFO) — decision-maker; vendor-preference-locked-in by Week 2; pleasant to work with but the analysis was theater for a decision already made.
- **Tom Akerly** (COO) — ally during evaluation; supportive of Infor LN before Holly's override; now living with the NetSuite reality.
- **Cassie Howell** (VP Manufacturing) — owned the traceability requirements; understood why Infor LN was right; has been quiet since the NetSuite decision, which I read as politically appropriate-but-uncomfortable.
